Figures 14 and 15a–c bring a second, geometry-based perspective to the same question, this time using four different linkage criteria on a representative sample (full-population dendrograms would be computationally impractical and visually unreadable at 32,000+ leaves). The Ward dendrogram (Figure 14) — generally the most informative for roughly spherical, similarly-sized clusters, which is the kind of structure a MinMax-scaled KMeans solution will tend to produce — shows a small number of well-separated high-level merges, consistent with a moderate number of natural groups rather than two or three large ones. The complete- and average-linkage trees (Figures 15a–b) tell a broadly similar story with somewhat different merge heights, while the single-linkage tree (Figure 15c) displays the classic “chaining” effect (long, staircase-like merges) that single linkage is known to produce on data without hard density gaps — a useful negative result, because it confirms that the customer base does not separate into a small number of extremely tight, isolated balls, but into softer, partially-overlapping behavioural groups.
