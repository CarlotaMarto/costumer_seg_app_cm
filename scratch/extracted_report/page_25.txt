Rather than trusting a single 2-D view (each projection technique can flatter or distort cluster geometry in its own way — PCA is linear and can compress non-linear structure, UMAP and t-SNE can each invent apparent separation that is not present in the original space), the notebook checks the same labelled solution through three different lenses. PCA (Figure 19), being a linear projection, shows partially overlapping clouds — which is expected and not a red flag, since the underlying feature space is nine-dimensional and a 2-D linear summary will necessarily compress some of that structure. UMAP (Figure 20) shows visibly tighter, more separated groupings, consistent with the silhouette evidence that the clusters are real but soft-edged rather than razor sharp. t-SNE (Figure 21) corroborates the UMAP picture with its own characteristic “island” layout.
