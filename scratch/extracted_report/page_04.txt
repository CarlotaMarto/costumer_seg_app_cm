1. Executive Summary This report documents and evaluates the full Customer Segmentation project built for the Machine Learning II course (Data Science Degree, NOVA IMS). The objective set out in the project statement was to use unsupervised learning to discover meaningful customer segments from two datasets — customer_info (33,038 customers with demographic, spending and engagement attributes) and customer_basket (100,000 sampled transactions with item-level detail) — and then translate those segments into concrete, creative marketing campaigns using association-rule mining on the basket data. The work is organised as a pipeline of six notebooks: an initial data audit (00_data_analysis), a preprocessing and feature-engineering stage (01_eda_preprocessing), a dedicated geographic exploration (02_eda_geographic), the clustering and validation notebook (03_clustering), the cluster-characterization and naming notebook (04_cluster_characterization), and finally the association-rules and campaign-design notebook (05_association_rules). Each stage exports clean, versioned CSV artefacts that the next stage consumes, and the heavy lifting is abstracted into four reusable utility modules (utils_eda.py, utils_clustering.py, utils_cluster_characterization.py, utils_association_rules.py), which is exactly the “DRY”, notebook-light approach the brief asks for. The final, exported solution is a KMeans model with k = 8 clusters, fitted on a MinMax-scaled feature space of nine spend-and-promotion variables (groceries excluded), after a deliberate “separate-then-reattach” outlier strategy: 1,023 atypical customers were modelled aside and reattached to their nearest centroid only after the centroids were learned on the remaining 32,015 “regular” customers. The eight segments — Vegetarians, Regulars, Wellness, Promoters, Loyalists, Families, Economizers and Techies — range from 9.4% to 19.5% of the customer base, a healthy balance that avoids one dominant catch-all group. The average silhouette of the final solution is 0.25, and the choice of k = 8 and of this particular feature space was made only after an unusually wide diagnostic search across scalers, feature sets, values of k, and four alternative clustering families (hierarchical, density-based DBSCAN, Self-Organising Maps, and a two-stage KMeans-then-Ward benchmark). Section by section, this report walks through every chart produced in the six notebooks, explaining what each one shows, how it was read by the project, and what it implies for the quality of the final segmentation. Overall, the project is methodologically strong: instead of presenting a single model as a foregone conclusion, it documents a chain of comparisons and explicitly rejected alternatives (e.g., a “petfood” feature variant that produced a cleaner pet-oriented segment but a slightly weaker overall geometry). This kind of documented decision trail is precisely what the evaluation rubric rewards under “Technical quality of the Segmentation” and “Interpretation of the Clusters. The report closes with a short list of items that still need attention before the June 9th deadline — most importantly, the executive report/app deliverable itself, the README, and the GitHub invitation to ivopbernardo — together with one substantive suggestion: curating the association-rule-based campaign list so that every promotion is narratively coherent with the segment it targets (a couple of the current top-lift rules pair oddly with their segment's defining behaviour). 2.
