Figure 18 is, in effect, a worked example that teaches the reader how to read Figure 17 — explicitly calling out the width (cluster size), length (how strongly its members belong), and any negative-valued slivers (members that are probably mis-clustered) as the three things to look for. Reading the grid this way, k = 8 stands out as the largest value of k at which every blade still reaches reasonably close to the mean line with no large negative regions — in other words, the most granular split the data supports without creating clusters that are mostly noise. This reasoning — “most granular split that is still defensible” rather than “highest score regardless of size” — is a mature way to choose k for a business segmentation, where eight distinguishable, addressable groups are also simply more useful to a marketing team than three or four very broad ones. The final configuration that emerges from this search is: KMeans, k = 8, MinMaxScaler, fitted on a nine-column feature set covering the customer's spend categories and promotion usage with groceries deliberately excluded (the “spend + promo, no groceries” set — see Section 6.6 for why groceries was dropped), trained on the 32,015 “regular” customers that remained after the 1,023 consensus outliers were separated out. This configuration achieves an average silhouette of 0.2516 — a moderate-but-respectable score for behavioural segmentation of real retail data (perfect separation is essentially never observed in this kind of human-behaviour clustering; scores in the 0.2–0.3 range are typical of usable, business-meaningful segmentations on noisy real-world features). 6.6 Validating the chosen solution: PCA, UMAP and t-SNE projections
