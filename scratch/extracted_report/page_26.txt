 Figure 23 — Continuation of the petfood-variant validation (additional projection / silhouette view). This is one of the most intellectually honest moments in the notebook. The team tries an alternative nine-feature set that swaps in lifetime_spend_petfood, and finds that it actually produces a slightly higher average silhouette (0.2653 vs. 0.2516) and a visibly cleaner, easy-to-name “pet owners” segment (Figures 22–23). A weaker analysis would simply have taken the higher number and moved on. Instead, the notebook weighs the trade-off explicitly: the petfood variant buys roughly 0.014 of silhouette at the cost of a feature that only a minority of the customer base ever purchases (most customers spend nothing on pet food, so the variable is highly zero-inflated and risks becoming a near-binary “owns a pet, yes/no” split that dominates the geometry rather than describing general spending behaviour). The team ultimately keeps the original “spend + promo, no groceries” set for the headline model, on the grounds that it produces more broadly interpretable, more evenly-populated segments — and keeps the petfood variant on record as a documented, defensible alternative rather than discarding the evidence. This kind of side-by-side, pros-and-cons comparison of two genuinely competitive options is exactly the sort of judgment call an evaluator wants to see argued, not hidden. 6.8 Why groceries was excluded from the feature set A short but important methodological note threaded through this part of the notebook: lifetime_spend_groceries is deliberately left out of the final feature set. Groceries are close to a universal purchase — almost every customer buys them, in large and fairly similar volumes — so including this variable tends to dominate the Euclidean distance with a “how much food does this household need” signal that drowns out the more discriminating, segment-defining categories (alcohol, electronics, video games, fish, hygiene, and so on). Removing it lets the more behaviourally distinctive categories drive the clustering geometry, which is precisely what produces segments that are easy to *name* (Vegetarians, Techies, Wellness, …) rather than segments that mostly just track household size.
