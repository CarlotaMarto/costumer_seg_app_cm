Figure 9 shows that some high values remain even after the conservative separation — which the notebook correctly frames as expected, not as a flaw: lifetime spending is naturally skewed, and the goal was never to flatten every univariate extreme, only to keep the small number of truly atypical customers from dominating the distance calculation. Figure 10, the correlation heatmap, is then used to avoid redundant features carrying duplicated weight in the clustering distance — the notebook notes, for instance, that year_first_transaction correlates strongly with the engineered tenure variable, and keeps only one of the pair in the final distance space. 4.7 Final exports: two parallel pipelines The notebook produces two alternative “clustering-ready” datasets rather than a single one: info_clustering_unscaled.csv (the separation + reattachment strategy described above, which keeps the regular customer base homogeneous for fitting) and info_clustering_capped.csv (an alternative where extreme values are clipped to IQR fences instead of being separated, so that every customer stays in the fitting population). The notebook is explicit about why this comparison belongs here and not in the clustering notebook: IQR fences must be computed on the full population — if they were computed after removing outliers, the fences would be tighter and would behave differently. A short capping-diagnostics table (Section 9.1) reports, per column, how many values were capped and by how much the maximum changed, which is exactly the kind of evidence an evaluator would want to see before trusting either pipeline. Both exports preserve the customer_id index, so the two strategies can be benchmarked apples-to-apples in Notebook 3 (see Section 6.2 of this report).
